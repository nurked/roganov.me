To perform this experiment, you'll need some equipment. And, potentially, a quantum computer. But in reality, it's not that complicated — you most likely already have everything you need.

To run the experiment, you need to follow one very specific instruction. Ready? Here it is:

"Think of a cat."

Congratulations. Done. You have just created a state of uncertainty in this universe.

The moment you imagined a cat, there was not a single being or mechanism in the world that could perceive the exact cat you're looking at.

No one, nothing — not even God Himself — has any clue which cat you are picturing. It exists because you know about it, but nothing in this universe knows what that cat looks like.

Was it an orange cat? Or black? Did this cat exist on planet Earth, or did you just make it up? And if it existed, is it still alive? Is it your cat? Or do you belong to this cat?

Maybe you're one of those people who immediately thought of a dog instead? Or do you just see a vague gray void? Or, like me — after reading way too much sci-fi — do you constantly imagine a six-legged cat made entirely of pure electricity?

Until you describe your thought to a chatbot or tell someone about this cat, two things remain true:

1. On this planet (and most likely in the entire universe), there is no way to obtain even the slightest bit of information about what you just thought of.

2. You are the only being in existence that can perceive this cat.

Here's the kicker — I've talked to several scientists while doing interviews for podcasts, and I've confirmed one simple truth: nobody knows how to extract thoughts from the brain.

It turns out that thinking about a cat triggers reactions in your brain, but the exact reactions are completely different for every individual body, and there is no way to determine whether you're picturing a cat or just thinking about eating chicken.

There are zero scientific studies proving that thoughts neatly arrange themselves in the brain like a well-organized library. But there are plenty of studies showing that thoughts do trigger certain brain reactions. In some cases, you can even observe that people have similar neural responses when thinking in broad categories.

And those articles claiming that someone "managed to train a system to recognize thoughts"? All of them turn out to be overblown nonsense.

(Interestingly enough, if you give a person a neuro interface, they can train their brain to send specific commands to control it — just like a newborn baby gradually learns to control their body, a person with a neuro interface can train their "brain muscle" to send signals in the right direction.)

But don't underestimate the potential impact a single thought in someone's head can have on the nature of this universe.

No, this isn't some wish-fulfillment seminar nonsense — your thought about a cat probably doesn't directly affect anything. But maybe, just maybe, one of you will decide to go adopt a cat from a shelter.

Or someone will get the idea to open a cat café.

And there are other minds out there whose thoughts can turn into spaceships, airplanes, ocean liners, or race cars.

And then there are those whose thoughts can kill.

### What About AI?

In the field of AI, I see real experts — people who truly understand how models work and don't get lost in speculative nonsense about "recreating human consciousness in a machine."

And then I also see the hype merchants — constantly shouting about how we are on the brink of "the next great evolutionary leap for humanity." (A leap we seem to take every 20–30 years, depending on how dramatic the tabloids feel at the time.)

Here's what I do know for sure:

I've been terrible at drawing since childhood.

In school, they literally gave me extra pocket money and A+ grades just to make sure I wouldn't draw anything. It was that bad. It's a form of suffering — for everyone involved.

But today, I can take any halfway decent LLM, and after a bit of prompting, I can make it generate an image that at least vaguely represents the cat I'm imagining.

Because I get to decide how to transmit the idea of an electric six-legged cat to you.

(And, as always, the LLM lies. Because it cannot create what it has never been trained on. And as you can see from this image — after twenty attempts, I still couldn't get it to understand what a six-legged cat is.)
